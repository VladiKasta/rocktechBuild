AGEING RESISTANCE

Long-term resistance is a key concern for fire protection products, as they must function throughout a building's lifespan. While aging effects on materials like plastics, paints, and metals can be simulated, no standard time-lapse tests exist for fire protection products. These products are made from various compounds, making it difficult to assess them under the same criteria.


To provide more reliable information, we do accelerate aging tests in collaboration with an independent institute. Based on these tests and industry experience, Rock Tech firestop systems are expected to last approximately 30 years from manufacturing.

Water resistance

Facade sealing is crucial for protecting buildings from the elements and ensuring long-term performance. High-quality sealants help maintain both aesthetic appeal and structural integrity. Water can enter buildings through several means, including:

Gravity

Water can flow downward and accumulate, especially on low-slope facades.

Kinetic Energy

High winds can force rainwater into gaps or cracks.

Surface Tension

Water droplets may penetrate the facade instead of flowing down.

Capillarity

Water can flow into small gaps through capillary action.

Air Current

Wind can drive water into cracks.

Differential Pressures

Differences in air pressure can push water through unsealed facades.

Smoke and Gas Tightness

Most fire-related deaths are caused by asphyxiation, where oxygen is replaced by carbon dioxide and toxic gases. Inhalation of hot gases also damages the lungs, preventing proper function. Fires can reach temperatures of 600-800°C, overwhelming the body’s ability to function. Additionally, smoke and gases travel quickly, filling rooms in seconds and making it hard to escape.


For this reason, fire tests under EN 1366 measure pressure changes in test furnaces and the gases that escape. A significant pressure drop indicates that firestopping products aren’t effectively containing the gases, compromising safety.

Sound Insulation

When installing floors, ceilings, windows, or outlets, it's important to consider how sound travels through the space. Acoustical sealants help improve sound quality and block unwanted noise, especially in areas with air leaks or vibrations. Key applications include:

Doors

Apply sealant around door edges to block sound leakage.

Drywall

Seal seams between drywall sheets to prevent sound transmission.

Windows

Remove molding to find gaps where sound escapes.

Floors

Floors can also transmit sound, so use soundproofing solutions.

Service Penetrations

Seal gaps around pipes, cables, or ducts to prevent noise.

Using acoustical sealants in these areas helps reduce noise entering or leaving your space.
